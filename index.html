About Me

Hi, my name is Ben Lipkin, and I am a current Research Associate in EvLab at MIT BCS and the McGovern Institute for Brain Research. Check out this site to learn more about my research, my interests, and how to get in touch with me.

Background
I completed my undergraduate studies at the University of Michigan in Ann Arbor, where I studied Neuroscience with a focus on Computation & Cognition. From 2018-2020, I worked with David Brang on a series of research and software development projects, spanning from visual object feature decoding, to the analysis of articulation network dynamics in glioma-infiltrated cortex, to the development of signal processing tools for the analysis of neural time-series data. After completing a thesis titled 'Decoding object color binding using multivariate pattern analysis' in early 2020, I graduated with high-honors, and relocated to Cambridge, MA. Since then, I have been working with Evelina Fedorenko on a range of projects related to the investigation of human brain and deep neural network (DNN) representations of language and other hierarchically structured systems, e.g. computer code, visual events, etc.

Research Statement
Through my research experience and independent study, I have started developing a series of driving questions that I hope to guide my research in the coming years. While the study of neuroscience used to be particularly focused on the investigation of a specific biological organ with a series of functions, the emergence of high-performing DNNs has provided an interesting new avenue for investigation. Abstracting from the focus on a particular biological system, there has emerged a new field of study on the class of more general systems, of which the brain and DNNs are instances, that are capable of expressing certain patterns of behavior and computations, from visual object detection to next-word prediction. This background motivates a series of broad driving research questions, about the nature of these systems, the behaviors and computations they share, and their differences.

1. What is the nature of object representation? How is this similar and different between the brain and DNNs? In the case of similarities, to what extent is this variance attributable to our shared 'training' experience, whereby the human brain and DNNs structure themselves to optimize for the constraints of the natural statistics of our environment? If we begin to manipulate the natural statistics of the environment during training, how does that affect the mutual information shared by brain and DNN representations? In the case of differences, how is this variance attributable to the constraints imposed by the system architecture and training objective?

2. Relatedly, to what extent is object representation 'amodal'? Do 'concepts' exist independent of stimulus medium? How does the brain implement this? Is there a 'symbolic hub', or are these representations distributed? What about in SOTA models learning shared representations of text and vision? To what extent do these bi-modal DNN representations map onto known brain networks?

3. Expanding beyond objects themselves, how are object relations represented, e.g. composition, action, and interaction? How are these represented across diverse media, including sentences vs. visual scenes? Is there global similarity in the representation of these rich hierarchical graph structures, or are those computations tied to the stimulus modality?

Next Steps
My goal is to continue exploring the world of academic science and to continue my learning and studying in a graduate research capacity. As such, I will be applying to PhD programs these next few months, to start in the fall of 2022.
