About Me

Hi, my name is Ben Lipkin, and I am a current PhD student and researcher in the Department of Brain & Cognitive Sciences (BCS) at the Massachusetts Institute of Technology (MIT). I am grateful for funding support from the Computationally-Enabled Integrative Neuroscience Training Program (CEIN) and an MIT Presidential Fellowship. Check out this site to learn more about my active research, published work, personal interests, and how to get in touch with me.

Background
I completed my undergraduate degree at the University of Michigan in Ann Arbor, where I studied Neuroscience with a focus on Computation & Cognition. From 2018-2020, I worked with David Brang on a series of research and software development projects, spanning cognitive neuroscience, biomedical engineering, and statistics. After completing my thesis on "Decoding object color binding using multivariate pattern analysis" in early 2020, I graduated with high-honors, and relocated to Cambridge, MA. I then spent several years working as a Research Associate with Evelina Fedorenko on a range of projects investigating human brain and artificial neural network (ANN) representations of language and other abstract hierarchically structured systems, from computer programming to recursive social reasoning. Through this work, I developed an interest in how the brain represents abstract properties (e.g., concepts and categories) and applies abstract structures (e.g., graphs, grammars, and programs) to solve tasks, and how we can go about modeling and safely endowing these skills in artificial systems.

Current Focus
For my doctoral research, I am working with a series of groups on a range of projects surrounding a common core theme: How do the brain and mind model the hierarchically structured representations of languages and programs, and for what objectives are such information scaffolding structures optimized?
